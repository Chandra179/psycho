Abstract

The present study examines the relationship between linguistic function-word usage and self-reported personality traits across a corpus of 10,487 blog authors. Building upon the theoretical framework established by Pennebaker and King (1999), we hypothesize that the relative frequency of articles, prepositions, and cognitive process words constitutes a reliable indicator of analytical thinking disposition, whereas pronoun density inversely correlates with formal reasoning patterns. Methodologically, we employ a regression-based approach wherein LIWC2015 category percentages serve as independent variables and NEO-PI-R domain scores constitute the dependent measures. Results indicate a Spearman correlation of r = 0.34 between article usage and Openness to Experience, consistent with prior findings by Yarkoni (2010). Furthermore, the composite index of certainty words and exclusive particles demonstrates a moderate positive association with Conscientiousness (r = 0.28, p < 0.001). Importantly, the interaction between first-person singular pronoun frequency and negative emotion word density accounts for 17% of the variance in Neuroticism scores. These findings contribute to the emerging literature on computational personality assessment by validating the predictive utility of function-word distributions independent of content-based semantic analysis. We discuss limitations including corpus homogeneity, the reliance on self-report criterion measures, and the potential confounding effect of genre conventions on linguistic feature distributions. Future research should investigate cross-linguistic generalizability and the temporal stability of these language-personality associations.

Introduction

The quantification of individual differences through linguistic analysis has emerged as a significant methodological paradigm within computational social science. While prior research has demonstrated consistent associations between lexical choice patterns and personality dimensions, the precise mechanisms through which function words encode dispositional information remain insufficiently theorized. This investigation addresses three primary objectives: first, to replicate and extend the findings of Yarkoni (2010) using an independent corpus; second, to decompose the relative contributions of content words versus function words to personality prediction accuracy; and third, to evaluate whether composite linguistic indices outperform individual category percentages in trait estimation.

Our theoretical orientation derives from the linguistic category model, which posits that function words operate as implicit markers of cognitive processing style. Articles and prepositions, for instance, require the speaker to engage in concrete categorical reasoning, thereby indexing an analytical cognitive orientation. Conversely, pronoun usage reflects attentional allocation to social versus self-referential content, with first-person singular pronouns indicating heightened self-focus. The present research operationalizes these theoretical constructs through a systematic quantitative methodology that integrates computational linguistics with established psychometric frameworks.

Method

Participants were recruited through an online platform and provided informed consent for the analysis of their publicly available blog content. The final sample consisted of 10,487 participants (62% female, mean age 34.2 years) who completed the full NEO-PI-R inventory and contributed a minimum of 5,000 words of blog text spanning a period of at least six months. Text preprocessing included removal of HTML markup, normalization of Unicode characters, and segmentation into sentences and paragraphs using a rule-based natural language processing pipeline. Linguistic feature extraction utilized a modified LIWC2015 dictionary augmented with empirically derived category extensions.

Statistical analysis proceeded in three stages. First, we computed zero-order correlations between each LIWC category and personality dimension. Second, we constructed hierarchical linear regression models entering demographic covariates in the first block, content-word categories in the second block, and function-word categories in the third block. Third, we evaluated composite indices derived from the linguistic summary variables described by Pennebaker et al. (2014), including analytical thinking, clout, authenticity, and emotional tone. Model comparison utilized adjusted R-squared values and Akaike Information Criterion differences.

Results

Descriptive statistics for the primary variables are presented in Table 1. The median blog word count was 12,347 (IQR = 8,210-19,455), with a mean dictionary coverage of 78.3% (SD = 6.2%). Consistent with prior research, function words accounted for approximately 55% of all tokens, with pronouns representing the largest function-word subcategory.

The hierarchical regression analyses revealed that function-word categories incrementally predicted personality variance beyond content-word categories across all five dimensions. The magnitude of incremental prediction was largest for Openness (ΔR² = 0.09) and smallest for Agreeableness (ΔR² = 0.03). Composite linguistic indices demonstrated superior predictive performance relative to individual category percentages for three of five dimensions, with the analytical thinking index emerging as the strongest single predictor of Openness (β = 0.31).

Table 2 presents the full correlation matrix between linguistic categories and personality dimensions. Notable findings include the negative association between first-person singular pronouns and Conscientiousness (r = -0.19), the positive association between positive emotion words and Extraversion (r = 0.24), and the substantial correlation between cognitive process words and Openness (r = 0.34). Interaction analyses revealed that the combination of high negative emotion word usage and high first-person singular pronoun frequency produced the strongest prediction of Neuroticism (partial r = -0.41 after controlling for demographic variables).

Discussion

These findings provide robust replication of the core language-personality associations documented in the extant literature while extending this work through the systematic comparison of individual categories versus composite indices. The superior predictive performance of composite linguistic summary variables, particularly the analytical thinking index, suggests that these higher-order linguistic constructs capture dispositional information that is distributed across multiple lexical categories rather than concentrated within any single category. This finding has both theoretical implications for models of language production and practical implications for the design of automated personality assessment systems.

Several limitations warrant consideration. First, the exclusive reliance on blog text may constrain the generalizability of these findings to other communicative contexts, as genre conventions demonstrably influence linguistic feature distributions independent of author disposition. Second, the cross-sectional design precludes causal inference regarding the directionality of language-personality associations. Third, the use of a convenience sample limits demographic representativeness, particularly with respect to educational attainment and socioeconomic status. Future investigations should employ longitudinal designs, incorporate multimodal text sources, and validate findings across linguistically and culturally diverse populations.

Conclusion

This investigation demonstrates that function-word distributions constitute reliable and valid indicators of personality dimensions, with composite linguistic indices offering incremental predictive utility beyond individual lexical categories. The findings support the theoretical proposition that linguistic style, operationalized through function-word frequency patterns, encodes dispositional information that is accessible through automated computational analysis. These results contribute to the methodological foundation for developing transparent, auditable personality assessment systems that operate exclusively on linguistic evidence, consistent with the broader movement toward explainable artificial intelligence in psychological measurement.
